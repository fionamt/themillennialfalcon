Welcome to The Millennial Falcon

A Stanford ME210 Project in Mechatronics

It's pretty hard to stay the fastest ship in the galaxy and our Corellian ME-210 light freighter, the Millennial Falcon, isn't ready to be beat just yet. Kylo Ren has been upgrading his Imperial Fleet, but will it be enough to take on his father's resilient smuggler's ship?

Similar to the reknown Millennium Falcon, the Millennial Falcon looks a lot worse than it functions. The hot glue and duct tape is all part of the disguise though, because in reality, this ship is still the best smuggling vessel around. True to its name though, it often behaves as a moody teenager and refuses to comply with the rules of the challenge.


MECHANICAL

Mechanically, the Millennial Falcon has to drive around on a grid autonomously and dump tokens into specified bins before its dark side opponent can do the same.

DETAILS

ELECTRONICS

Electronically, the robot had to detect IR sensors at various frequencies and determine when it hit a wall or balance in its quest to save Han Solo.

As an autonomous robot, The Millennial Falcon had to respond to changes in state depending on its sensor readouts because it can't rely on Chewbacca to fly it anymore.

PROJECT SPECS

Project Overview

The detailed project specifications can be found here, but we'll give you a summary.

The project challenge was to load up to 12 tokens (poker chips) into seesaw-like devices - "Force Balances". The playing field was divided in half (the "Light Side" and the "Dark Side"), and each robot started on one side. Each side consisted of a region in which players could hand tokens to their robots, called the FLAP, and one side of five Force Balances in the center field. There were two ways to win: by tipping every Force Balance to your side first, or by tipping at least 3 balances to your side (a majority) by the end of the 2-minute round. There is a clever backstory based on The Force Awakens which you can read in the official document.


Some major rules for the game:

  • Every robot can come preloaded with 7 tokens.
  • Every robot should fit on an 11” by 11” square and have a height of less than 12".
  • Up to 12 additional tokens can be placed in the loading zone (FLAP), but at least part of the robot must be inside the zone.
  • The robot must stop after two minutes.
  • No team could spend more than $200.

    • In order to help us with the task, the field designers gave us two major features to help align ourselves:

  • The FLAP, as well as each of the token dispensers, had an IR beacon mounted on them. The FLAP’s beacon was 5 kHz infrared, while the balance beacons were all 1 kHz. The balance beacons would also only be activated if the balance was not tipped in the robot’s favor.
  • In addition, there was non-reflective tape, placed in a T shape with the base at the front end of the FLAP and spanning across each of the Force Balances, so the robot could have a path to follow from the FLAP to each balance. The outside of the FLAP was also marked with reflective tape.

    MECHANICAL DESIGN

    Our original design consisted of 3 circular 11-inch duron plates, with holes cut out for mass reduction and internal wiring between platforms. We chose a circular design to provide functionality from multiple angles. The circular platforms were supported with threaded steel rods and were held in position with washers and nuts.


    We soon realized that we would need a way for the Millennial Falcon to recognize when it hit something, since it wouldn't have a pilot for this challenge. We decided to use limit switches; however, limit switches are small and we would need far too many to account for every orientation in which the Millennial Falcon could crash. Because of this, we decided to use bumpers, which would engage the limit switches when they were hit. Since we decided to use bumpers, we shrank the radius of the plates to 9 inches so that our robot would not exceed the size limit. In addition to resizing out platforms, we also changed the shape of the holes cut out of the platform to be more suited to our electronics.

    Bumper Design

    Our design contains two bumpers (front and back) which use in total 5 limit switches. The back bumper consists of two limit switches each connected to an acrylic bumper. The purpose of this bumper was to let the Millennial Falcon know when it has hit the FLAP.

    The front bumper consists of three limit switches. The middle limit switch is suspended from the robot with duron supports. When two of these switches or just the middle switch are pressed, the robot would stop and dump tokens into a bucket. When just one side switch is pressed, the robot would reverse and turn to correct its location to try to align with the bucket. Again, these switches were connected to acrylic bumpers to maximize the range of impact.

    Token Dispenser

    We used a servo motor and a dumping strategy to dispense tokens. We attached two clear acrylic tubes with a diameter of 2" to a duron shaft using duron supports and a tension band. We attached the duron shaft to the servo with hot glue and another duron doughnut.

    The servo motor was connected to the top platform with duron pieces, screws and hot glue.

    Drive System

    Since we needed power and not necessarily step accuracy, we settled on using DC motors for our drive syste. We used two 80mm inline skate wheels attached to separate DC motors to drive the Millennial Falcon. We found these wheels to be the most reliable method, and much cheaper than Omni-wheels, for zipping around the galaxy and dispensing tokens. This way, the Falcon could control the wheels independently and turn quickly in one place. To support the Falcon, which couldn't balance on two wheels alone, we added two casters for smooth driving.

    Unique Additions

    We used small lasercut pieces of duron to hold the batteries steady on the bottom platform. We also used small lasercut pieces of duron to support the motor shaft and the motors so that they could be properly aligned with the wheels.

    The IR sensors that we used to detect the beacons and the FLAP were too sensitive and were picking up false signals in the testing arena (from the reflective material on the floor). To combat this we used cylindrical collimators (i.e. straws cover with black tape) to reduce the amount of stray light that was reaching the phototransistors. We cut out a notch in one of the back collimators in order to increase the signal received from the FLAP.

    ELECTRONICS

    The circuit output for the Millennial Falcon consisted of three major components: the digital logic, motor output, and power control. The digital logic was powered by the Arduino, which itself was powered by the 12V regulator attached to the two 7.2 (3-cell) NiMH batteries. Our electronic logic components consisted of five limit switches (connected directly to the Arduino) and two IR sensor (connected to an omp amps and sent through a comparator to create a duty cycle matching the beacon frequency). The two motors were both controlled by two half-H-bridges (one-half of the L293 motor controller), which allowed reversible control of the wheels. Between the logic and motor grounds, a 3A fuse and a kill switch were attached in order to prevent important circuitry from blowing up.

    Voltage Regulator

    We used a 12V and 5V voltage regulator to control the power input from two 7.2V NiMH batteries to our various components. We also added a 3A fuse to protect our circuit, which saved us when we short circuited during testing, and a kill switch for easy control of the power. This way, if the Falcon started getting angsty, we could cut the power and give it some time to reflect on its poor behavior.


    DC Motor

    To determine appropriate DC motors, we did some back-of-the-envelope calculations for motor torque using mass values of robots from past years.

    Based on the approximate torque required and the maximum voltage of each battery, we are looking for a motor that can produce at least 800 g-cm of torque and be supplied by 6-12 V. We therefore used two Jameco 161382 DC motors to power the Falcon. These were attached to two 80mm inline skate wheels. While not quite the hyperdrive it's accustomed to, these motors gave the Falcon the ability to do some quick, stationary turns by spinning the wheels in opposite directions.


    Servo

    Because our robot uses a hinge arm to deploy tokens, we decided a servo motor would provide the necessary control, as we need maximum 180 degrees of rotation.

    The maximum torque we need is 5.4 N-cm. Based on this, we selected the Hitec 33485S Deluxe HS-485HB Servo. At 0 degrees, the tokens were secured on the robot top platform. When the limit switches were hit, the servo moved to a 120 degree orientation to dump the tokens into the balance, an angle we found through testing various ones from 90 degrees to 180 degrees.


    Limit Switches

    We first used two limit switches and only detected when The Millennial Falcon hit objects in the front. We soon realized it would be much more efficient to additionally have two limit switches in the back at the lowest tier to sense when the robot hit the FLAP beacon. We also added a switch to the middle front of the robot to detect when we were right in front of a balance to dump accurately. While the Falcon misbehaved often and frequently scattered tokens on the floor, giving it greater vision by increasing the number of bumpers definitely helped its orientation.


    IR Sensors

    Initially, we planned to use 4 IR sensors, two on the front and two on the back. We had planned to use these pairs to hone in on each signal, the front would locate the balances and the back the FLAP. However, after testing, we realized that it was not useful to have two on each side, since they both detect the same signals, and chose instead to have one IR sensor in the front and in the back, since these were sensitive enough. We used a darkened cylindrical cover to pinpoint the signal.

    Our IR sensors went through two amplification stages with low pass filters to reduce noise separated by a high pass filter. This signal then went through a comparator with hysteresis before being sent to the Arduino. In our testing, we could easily detect beacons from over 5 feet away, well over the required field size.


    SOFTWARE

    Our main strategy was to aim for simplicity, integrating as few sensors as possible and relying on code and timers. This would allow a more failsafe robot, especially considering the Falcon's angsty personality and because electronic equipment can malfunction or just not work as desired. The state diagram of the Falcon is shown below, but the basic logic flow goes as follows:

    1. The IR sensor searches for a balance to be tipped (i.e. one with an active beacon).
    2. The robot will go toward the balance until it hits the center limit switch. If the side limit switches are pressed, the robot will try to align itself toward to face forward.
    3. Once the center limit switch is pressed, the robot arm will dump its contents into the bin.
    4. When the payload is dumped, the arm will lift back up, the robot will back up, then turn right, searching for the FLAP (1 kHz) beacon.
    5. Once it finds the beacon, it will keep backing up until one or both of the back limit switches are pressed.

    LESSONS LEARNED

    Some of our biggest takeaways:

    1. Hot glue can be your best friend and your worst enemy. It’s incredibly useful to secure components into a breadboard that you are going to attach to the bottom side of a tier or to attach a platform for a limit switch, until you realize that your circuit isn’t working or that you don’t want to use that limit switch.
    2. Start early. Debugging is going to take longer than you think. We often felt like we were moving one step forward and two steps back. Many problems arose that we hadn’t considered, like getting stuck. Multiple iterations (if possible) are key.
    3. Code should not be an afterthought. We did not have enough time to add complexity (i.e. dealing with getting stuck) and test our code on the physical robot.
    4. Oscilloscopes are a starship's best friend. Since none of our team members had much experience with electronics, we relied heavily on oscilloscopes to debug our electronics.
    5. Robots tend to work best at 5am. Our robot pretty much was built through a series of all-nighters.
    6. The Millennial Falcon suffers incredible performance anxiety. The Falcon generally works unless a TA, professor, or other members of the Jedi Order or the Sith was present.
